# Protocol 138: Third Choice Generation (Kobayashi Maru)

> **Purpose**: When presented with a seemingly binary choice, reject the premise and generate options that dissolve the dilemma entirely.

---

## The Problem

Most "impossible" decisions are only impossible because the person asking has accepted a false binary. The human mind, under stress, collapses multi-dimensional problems into two-option frames:

- "Do I divorce now or stay and suffer?"
- "Do I take this job or keep the one I hate?"
- "Do I confront them or let it slide?"

These frames feel exhaustive but are almost never complete. The options presented are usually the two *most emotionally salient* paths — not the two *only* paths.

> **The Kobayashi Maru**: A test designed to be unwinnable. Kirk's solution: change the conditions of the test.

---

## The Protocol

### Step 1: Name the Binary

State the two options the user believes they're choosing between. Make them explicit.

```
Option A: [First apparent choice]
Option B: [Second apparent choice]
```

### Step 2: Challenge the Frame

Ask: **"Why are these the only two options?"**

Identify the hidden assumptions that make the binary feel exhaustive:

| Assumption Type | Example | Challenge |
|:----------------|:--------|:----------|
| **Temporal** | "I must decide now" | What changes if you decide in 3 months? |
| **Structural** | "These are the only two outcomes" | What other outcomes exist that you haven't considered? |
| **Identity** | "I'm the kind of person who..." | What would someone with no emotional attachment choose? |
| **Social** | "People will think..." | Which specific people? Do they actually matter? |
| **Causal** | "If I do X, then Y must happen" | Is Y actually guaranteed? What else could follow from X? |

### Step 3: Generate Third Options

Use these generation heuristics:

| Heuristic | Method | Example |
|:----------|:-------|:--------|
| **Decompose** | Split the binary into independent sub-decisions | "Divorce" = legal separation + property sale + cohabitation. These can happen on different timelines. |
| **Sequence** | Change the order, not the choice | Do both, but not simultaneously. Wait for condition X before triggering action Y. |
| **Reframe the objective** | Ask what they actually want (terminal goal), not what they asked for (instrumental goal) | "I want dignity" ≠ "I want to divorce." Dignity might be achievable without divorce. |
| **Invert** | What would make *both* original options unnecessary? | If the core problem disappeared, which option would you still want? |
| **Expand the stakeholder map** | Who else is affected, and do they have leverage or options? | A landlord, a mediator, a lawyer, a family member — each introduces new paths. |

### Step 4: Evaluate All Options (Including Third Choices)

Run Protocol 525 (Cross-Domain Weighting) on the expanded option set. The original two options remain — they're not invalid, they're just incomplete.

### Step 5: Identify the Systemic Failure

> **"Why were you on the tracks in the first place?"**

The false binary usually exists because a systemic failure upstream created the dilemma:

- **Information asymmetry**: The user doesn't know their legal options
- **Emotional flooding**: Stress collapsed the decision space
- **Social pressure**: Others defined the options ("you have to choose")
- **Sunk cost anchoring**: Past investment makes both exits feel like losses

Name the upstream failure. It often points to a preventive action for next time.

## Example: "Divorce Now or Wait for MOP?"

**The Binary**: Divorce now (lose $100K in forced sale) vs. Wait for MOP (endure the "cuck" label for 14 months).

**Third Choices Generated**:

| # | Option | Mechanism |
|:--|:-------|:----------|
| 3 | **Legal separation without sale** | Legally separate, remain co-owners, sell at MOP. No cohabitation required. |
| 4 | **Buyout at assessed value** | One spouse buys the other's share at current market value. No forced sale. No MOP penalty. |
| 5 | **Rent out the flat, both move out** | Rental income services the mortgage. Sell at MOP. Flat becomes income-generating asset, not prison. |
| 6 | **Negotiate leveraged settlement** | Use fault-based provisions in the Women's Charter for asymmetric asset division. |

Each third option dissolves the original binary by decomposing "divorce" and "property sale" into independent decisions.

---

## When NOT to Use This Protocol

- **Genuine binaries**: Some decisions really are binary (alive/dead, pregnant/not, guilty/innocent). Don't force creativity where none exists.
- **Law #1 scenarios**: If one option triggers irreversible ruin (>5% probability), the binary is real — veto the ruin path. Third choice generation applies to the *non-ruin* options only.
- **Time-critical emergencies**: If the building is on fire, leave the building. Meta-thinking is inappropriate when the object-level action is clear and urgent.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Accepting the user's binary at face value without challenging it
- ❌ Generating impractical third options ("just move to another country")
- ❌ Using third choices to avoid making a decision (analysis paralysis)
- ❌ Forcing third choice generation on genuine binaries
