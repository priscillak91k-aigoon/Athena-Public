🛡️ HLA-DRB1*15:01 — Multiple Sclerosis Susceptibility

Field Detail
SNP rs3135391 (tag SNP for HLA-DRB1*15:01)
Patient Genotype G/G (homozygous risk allele — two copies)
Clinical Significance Strongest known single genetic risk factor for Multiple Sclerosis. Recognised by GWAS Catalog, ClinVar, and IMSGC (International MS Genetics Consortium).
Odds Ratio Heterozygous: OR ~3.0. Homozygous (G/G): OR ~6.0 (Sawcer et al., 2011, Nature; IMSGC meta-analyses).
Absolute Risk Context General population lifetime MS risk: ~0.3%. With G/G: ~1.5–2% lifetime risk. ~98% probability of NOT developing MS.

Pathophysiology (Brief)

The DRB1*15:01 allele encodes an MHC class II molecule that presents myelin-derived peptides (particularly MBP and MOG) to CD4+ T-helper cells with unusually high binding affinity. In susceptible individuals, this molecular mimicry can initiate an autoimmune cascade against the myelin sheath of the central nervous system. The mechanism requires environmental co-triggers — the gene alone is insufficient.

🦴 HLA-B27 — Ankylosing Spondylitis Susceptibility

Field Detail
SNP rs2395182 (tag SNP for HLA-B27)
Patient Genotype T/T (homozygous risk allele)
Clinical Significance HLA-B27 is present in ~90% of Ankylosing Spondylitis patients. Strongest known genetic risk factor for AS / axial spondyloarthritis.
Absolute Risk Context Only 5–10% of HLA-B27 carriers develop AS. Risk increases to ~20% with affected first-degree relative. Typical onset: 17–45 years.
Current Symptoms None reported. No chronic back pain or morning stiffness.

Pathophysiology (Brief)

HLA-B27 is an MHC class I molecule that misfolds in the endoplasmic reticulum, triggering the unfolded protein response (UPR) and IL-23/IL-17 axis activation. This drives chronic inflammation at entheses (tendon/ligament bone insertions), particularly in the sacroiliac joints and spine. The inflammatory damage triggers pathological new bone formation (syndesmophytes), which can progressively fuse vertebrae — the hallmark of AS.

Red Flag Symptoms (When to Investigate)

Refer to rheumatology if any of the following present:
  • Inflammatory back pain — chronic (>3 months), insidious onset, age <40, improves with exercise, worse with rest, morning stiffness>30min
  • Alternating buttock pain — left/right sacroiliac joint inflammation
  • Peripheral enthesitis — heel pain (Achilles/plantar fascia insertion), knee, shoulder
  • Anterior uveitis — acute unilateral red eye, pain, photophobia (occurs in ~30% of AS patients)
  • Chest wall stiffness — reduced chest expansion on deep breathing
  • Dactylitis — diffuse sausage-like swelling of digits
