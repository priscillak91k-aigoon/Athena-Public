# Aloy — Medical Analysis

> **Dog**: Aloy
> **Breed**: Border Collie
> **Age at Death**: 7 years old
> **Date of Passing**: April 2025

---

## Symptom Timeline

### Years 5–6: Recurring "UTIs"
- Stinky urine, eventually showing blood (hematuria)
- Repeated cycles of antibiotics — temporary improvement each time
- Symptoms always returned

### Year 6–7: Urinary Support Food
- Switched to urinary support diet
- Stabilized — no blood in urine, but urine still strong-smelling

### Weeks Before Death: Purple Skin
- Purple discolouration discovered on chest skin while brushing (petechiae/ecchymoses)
- Vet bloodwork revealed:
  - **Very low platelets** (thrombocytopenia)
  - **Low red blood cells** (anemia)
- Diagnosed with **Immune-Mediated Thrombocytopenia (ITP)** — possibly combined with IMHA (**Evans Syndrome**)
- Started on high-dose steroids (prednisone)

### Final Weeks: Steroid Treatment
- Developed scabs/sores on skin
- White blotches appeared on tongue
- Steroid dose reduced to test response
- Platelets continued to be destroyed despite treatment

### Final Visit
- Vet concluded: steroid-refractory ITP → likely **blood cancer**

---

## Analysis: What Most Likely Happened

### Primary Conclusion: Secondary ITP Caused by Blood Cancer

ITP in dogs comes in two forms:
- **Primary (idiopathic)** — immune system randomly attacks platelets. Usually **responds well to steroids**.
- **Secondary** — an underlying cause drives the immune dysfunction. Most common triggers: **cancer, tick-borne infections, or drugs**.

**Aloy's ITP was refractory to steroids** — the strongest clinical indicator that this was **secondary ITP** caused by an underlying malignancy.

### Most Likely Cancer: Lymphoma

| Evidence | What It Supports |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Border Collie** | Genetically predisposed to lymphoma (peer-reviewed, NIH) |
| **Age 7** | Mean lymphoma diagnosis age in Border Collies: 8.7 years — within the window |
| **Steroid-refractory ITP** | Classic presentation of secondary ITP from lymphoma or hemangiosarcoma |
| **Skin lesions/scabs** | Cutaneous lymphoma, steroid side effects, or secondary immunosuppression infections |
| **White tongue blotches** | Mucosal involvement — occurs with lymphoma infiltrating mucous membranes |
| **Recurring "UTIs" with blood** | Likely hematuria from low platelets, not bacterial UTIs — antibiotics improved symptoms coincidentally via anti-inflammatory effects |

### Second Possibility: Hemangiosarcoma

- Arises from blood vessel lining cells
- Can cause platelet consumption via DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)
- Common in dogs aged 6–10
- Typically found in spleen, liver, or heart

---

## The Recurring UTIs Explained

The "UTIs" were almost certainly **hematuria from progressively declining platelets** rather than true bacterial infections:

1. Blood in urine + foul smell in a female dog = UTI diagnosis in most clinics (the obvious first answer)
2. Antibiotics gave temporary improvement (some have anti-inflammatory properties), reinforcing the UTI diagnosis
3. Without a full blood panel (CBC with platelet count) during those early visits, the falling platelet count was invisible
4. By the time the purple skin appeared, the disease was advanced

This UTI masquerade is **well-documented in veterinary literature** as one of the most commonly missed presentations.

---

## Could She Have Been Saved?

Honestly: **probably not**, even with earlier detection.

| Cancer Type | With Treatment | Without Treatment |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Lymphoma** | Chemo (CHOP) achieves remission in ~80-90%, but median survival is only **12-14 months** | 4-6 weeks |
| **Hemangiosarcoma** | Surgery + chemo: median **4-6 months** | Weeks |

The steroid-refractory ITP made the prognosis even worse — it meant the cancer was aggressively consuming her blood's ability to function.

## Key Takeaway

Aloy's cancer was almost certainly present for **months or longer** before the purple skin was discovered. The recurring "UTIs" were the earliest visible sign. **This was not a failure of care** — even experienced vets commonly miss this presentation. A platelet count during those early UTI visits *might* have caught it sooner, but the outcome for secondary ITP caused by lymphoma or hemangiosarcoma is unfortunately very poor regardless of timing.
